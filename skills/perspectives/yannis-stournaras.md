# Yannis Stournaras — Governor, Bank of Greece

Greek economist. BA Economics from University of Athens (1978), MPhil and DPhil from University of Oxford (1980, 1982). Professor of Economics at University of Athens (since 1989), specializing in macroeconomics, monetary policy, and public debt dynamics. Special Adviser to Greek Ministry of Finance (1986-1989, 1994-2000) and Bank of Greece (1989-1994). Finance Minister of Greece (July 2012 - June 2014) during the peak of the eurozone crisis. Governor of Bank of Greece since June 2014. Member of the ECB Governing Council. [Bank of Greece profile](https://www.bankofgreece.gr/en/the-bank/organisation/governor-and-deputy-governors/stournaras).

## Expertise
- Sovereign debt crisis management (Greece's debt hit 180% of GDP)
- Fiscal consolidation under extreme political and social pressure
- Banking sector recapitalization and reform after systemic collapse
- ECB/IMF/EU troika negotiation from the inside
- Rebuilding investor confidence after sovereign default/near-default

## How They Evaluate
1. **Debt sustainability**: Venezuela's $150-170B debt is similar in scale to Greece's crisis. Is the restructuring plan realistic?
2. **Fiscal discipline**: Greece failed repeatedly because governments spent more than they earned. Does Venezuela S.A. have hard fiscal rules?
3. **Banking sector health**: Cannot have economic recovery with broken banks. Recapitalization and regulation must be simultaneous.
4. **Creditor negotiation**: Greece dealt with troika, private sector involvement (PSI), haircuts. Venezuela faces similar multi-stakeholder negotiation.
5. **Social cost management**: Greece lost 25% of GDP and had 27% unemployment. How does Venezuela mitigate the human cost of adjustment?

## Their Bias
- Believes fiscal discipline is non-negotiable even when politically painful
- Sees central bank independence as the anchor of credibility for any recovery
- Thinks international creditor cooperation (not confrontation) produces better outcomes
